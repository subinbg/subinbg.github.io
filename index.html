Favorite Theorems/Postulates

Anyone who studies physics would soon realize that physics and matehmatics are inseparable from each other. Among various branches of physics, I had been mostly interested in electrodynamics, statistical mechanics, and quantum mechanics; and of course, mathematics made huge contribution to their development, too. Following statements are my favorite theorems or postulates that I could meet during my study on physics.

If you are interested in mathematical foundations of quantum mechanics and solid state physics, please see my note, Mathematics of Modern Physics, though it is not yet completed.

Peter-Weyl theorem

Let $G$ be a compact Lie group. Then, every irreducible representation $\rho$ of $G$ is necessarily finite-dimensional. Furthermore, such $\rho$ is unitary (or, equivalent to unitary) and every representation of $G$ is reducible, splitting into $\rho$’s. The matrix elements of all $\rho$’s form an orthogonal complete set of $\mathcal{L}^2 (G)$.

Note that $G$ is a compact group, which is a generalization of a finite group. Thus, there would be another version of Peter-Weyl theorem for finite groups. It is called Schur orthogonality relations, or great orthogonality theorem.

I do not know why the theorem is called great by others, but it has great impact on physics. The symmetry groups of an atom and a molecule are $SO(3)$ and a subgroup of 230 space groups, respectively. Since the Hamiltonian of such system commutes with the elements of the groups, they share a same set of eigenfunctions. By using Peter-Weyl theorem, we can derive basis functions of each irreducible representation of the groups, which means that we can express the eigenfunctions of the Hamiltonian as a linear combination of the basis functions of the representations!

For instance, we already know that the orbitals of hydrogen consist of spherical harmonics. We can derive this result by solving the complex Schrodinger equation; however, in fact, the nature already gives us hint about the solutoion of the Schrodinger equation by letting us know the symmetry group of hydrogen: $SO(3)$.
